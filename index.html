Year 4 is designed as clinical immersion. Your learning is built around real surgical experiences — developing recognition, diagnosis, and clinical reasoning across all specialties to prepare you for practice as an intern caring for surgical patients.

How to Learn

Approach to Learning

📋
01 / 06

Develop a Strong Study Plan

The provided example schedule (Table 1) integrates the overarching principles with specialty surgical topics — adapt it based on your clinical rotations, other commitments, and learning needs. Do not rely on last-minute revision; there is too much to cover. You should have a mix of different study techniques and resources that you draw on.

🏥
02 / 06

Deliberately Make Learning Opportunities

Use the 'On the Wards' section to cover key competencies in clinical environments. Go into ward rounds and clinics with purpose — set yourself goals each day (e.g. interpreting AXRs or diabetes management in surgical patients) and then discuss with the team. Many acute or serious surgical conditions are rare, so your reading must cover these. You may not have rotations in all specialties — seek common principles across surgery and supplement with focused didactic learning.

📝
03 / 06

Use WBAs as Learning Opportunities

Long cases are great case-based learning — read up about the patient's condition and identify key overarching principles to explore (e.g. pain management in a patient with long-term opioid use). Use handover WBAs for microlearning (discuss postoperative fever) and blood round WBAs to discuss postoperative anaemia. The viva requires practice in clinical history taking, interpretation of symptoms, signs and investigations, and development of management plans. The best practice is being on the wards and in theatres, talking with patients, nurses, and the surgical team.

💻
04 / 06

Make Efficient Use of Canvas

Canvas resources supplement ward-based and self-directed study and clarify areas where students often have difficulty — they are not exhaustive. The surgery assessment blueprint shows how assessment is structured across specialties. The 'Unscrubbed Podcasts' are interviews with junior doctors — great to listen to during your commute. Canvas also links Year 2 lectures from the GEL blocks for revision (deliberately not repeated in Year 4). Textbooks are available as ebooks from the USYD library (no purchase needed) with high-yield chapter descriptions.

🎓
05 / 06

Use Your Clinical Tutorials

Clinical Scenario Tutorials give you the opportunity to discuss more detailed aspects of surgical management and cover the overarching principles of surgery — ask questions and clarify approaches to common perioperative issues. Bedside clinical history and examination tutorials are a chance to practise the skills you need for your viva exams. Use your tutor's knowledge to develop your clinical reasoning skills.

🤖
06 / 06

Web Resources, Flash Cards & AI

Flash cards help with rote learning for KAT exam MCQs but not clinical reasoning (which is what the Viva tests) — because language is provided to you, you get less practice speaking and explaining concepts. Use other modalities alongside them. AI (ChatGPT, CoPilot) can be helpful for practice Viva and MCQ questions but can lead you astray — use cautiously and do not rely on it as a primary source. Geeky Medics (clinical examination guides), TeachMeSurgery (Key Points), eMedici, and AccessSurgery (MCQs and infographics) are excellent; free content is very good. Advanced features often require a subscription — Year 4 Surgery requirements do not require access and it is not necessary to pay. SUMS has an institutional login trial for 2026.

Vascular & Cardiothoracic Surgery

Curriculum Topics
⚡ Acute Surgical Conditions
  • Ruptured AAA
  • Acute Aortic Syndromes (dissection, penetrating ulcer)
  • Acute Limb Ischaemia
  • Deep venous thrombosis
Vascular Diseases
  • Peripheral artery disease (atherosclerosis related PAD including intermittent claudication and chronic-limb threatening ischaemia)
  • Aortic aneurysm
  • Diabetes-related foot disease (ulcers, sepsis, osteomyelitis)
  • Venous disease (superficial venous disease and varicose veins, venous ulcers, deep venous disease)
  • Carotid stenosis
Cardiothoracic Surgery
  • Surgical approaches to ischaemic heart disease
  • Valvular surgery
  • Pneumothorax
  • Lung cancer
  • Lung abscess / empyema

On the Wards

Clinical Competencies
Patients presenting with leg pain ▾
Differentiate between acute and chronic arterial ischaemia, venous insufficiency, neurological and musculoskeletal causes of leg pain.
Lower limb swelling ▾
Recognise signs and symptoms of venous insufficiency, venous thrombosis and metabolic causes of peripheral oedema.
Lower limb / foot ulceration ▾
Identify common causes of ulcers including arterial, venous and neuroischaemic ulcers related to diabetes; recognise superimposed infection with cellulitis or osteomyelitis; when surgery is indicated; initiate appropriate wound care.
Aortic aneurysm or dissection ▾
Recognise acute symptomatic and asymptomatic presentations; screening and investigation; thresholds for treatment; common perioperative management issues.
Ischaemic heart disease — coronary surgery ▾
Patients with ischaemic heart disease in whom surgery for coronary disease is considered: potential graft conduits; pre-surgical work up and investigations.

Curriculum Topics

  • Acute extremity fracture
  • Acute lacerations and tendon injuries
  • Wound closure
Orthopaedic Conditions
  • Surgical management of osteoarthritis (hip and knee)
  • Common shoulder conditions (including dislocation)
  • Bone and joint infections (septic joint, osteomyelitis)
  • Complications of fractures (non-union, nerve injuries, bleeding, compartment syndrome, contractures)
Hand Surgery
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Hand injuries, lacerations, tendon disruption
Plastics & Burns
  • Common reconstructive surgical techniques
  • Wound healing
  • Skin cancers (SCC/BCC, Melanoma)
  • Benign subcutaneous lumps (lipoma, sebaceous cysts, ganglion)
  • Subcutaneous abscesses
  • Soft tissue tumours (including sarcomas)
  • Acute and longer-term management of burns
  • Wound closure and complications of healing
  • Acute management of burns

On the Wards

Clinical Competencies
Interpreting an x-ray to describe a fracture or dislocation ▾
Including describing relevant anatomy, potential complications, and morphology of the fracture.
Recognise and manage complications of orthopaedic trauma ▾
Including compartment syndrome (also trauma surgery), nerve and vessel injury, open fracture, haemarthrosis, infections such as osteomyelitis.
Examine a hand, hip, knee, shoulder ▾
Including relevant anatomy and functional deficits.
Recovery after joint replacement ▾
Venous thromboembolism prevention; mobilisation; multidisciplinary approaches to rehabilitation including orthogeriatrics.
Benign vs malignant skin lesions ▾
Recognise melanoma; describe common surgical approaches to skin lesion excision and safe margins; recommend appropriate wound management.
Methods of skin grafting ▾
Split skin, full-thickness, flaps, and synthetic grafts.

On the Wards

Clinical Competencies
Interpretation of abdominal / chest x-rays ▾
Including recognition of common pathology such as perforated viscus, bowel obstruction.
Patient presenting with acute Cholecystitis / Cholangitis ▾

Patient presenting with Obstructive jaundice ▾
Including clinical history and physical examination; interpreting laboratory investigations including disordered coagulation and INR; ordering appropriate imaging.
Patient diagnosed with pancreatitis ▾
Key risk factors; fluid resuscitation/replacement; acute pain management; staging/severity of pancreatitis; longer-term sequalae after pancreatitis.
Groin hernia ▾
Recognise anatomical site/location of hernia; identification of complications of hernias including strangulation/incarceration; consider indication and timing of surgical options for hernia repair.

Perioperative Care

Overarching Principles of Surgery

Pre-operative Assessment & Risk

  • Identification of the high-risk surgical patient
  • Classification of perioperative risk using tools such as American Anaesthesiologist Score (ASA)
  • Approaches to pre-operative investigations
  • Diabetes management in the perioperative setting
  • Antithrombotic management in the perioperative setting
  • Management of patients with chronic pain in the pre-operative setting (including patients prescribed opioids)

Preparation for Surgery

  • Perioperative Fasting (including ERAS protocols and Sip-til-send)
  • Venous thromboembolism prophylaxis
  • Surgical antimicrobial prophylaxis
  • Preparation for theatre, informed consent, emergency bookings

Intraoperative Aspects

  • Surgical safety checklist and communication in theatre
  • Aseptic techniques and sterility
  • Local anaesthetic doses and administration
  • Surgical approaches and techniques for wound closure

Post-operative Management

  • Pain management (acute and chronic)
  • IV fluid management (resuscitation, replacement, maintenance)
  • Common perioperative complications (HAP, MI, delirium, urinary retention, ileus, DVT/PE)
  • Fever in post-operative patients
  • Post-operative anaemia, bleeding and massive transfusions
  • Perioperative nutrition
  • Rehabilitation, mobility and function after surgery
  • Role of other specialties/models of care: acute surgical teams, orthogeriatrics/surgical geriatrics, anaesthesia, acute and chronic pain teams, intensive care, rehabilitation medicine, allied health

On the Wards

  • Charting IV fluids for patients with surgical conditions
  • Management of postoperative pain: appropriate escalation of analgesics; modalities (oral, PCA, IV infusions, regional blocks); recognising common side-effects; safe prescribing practices
  • Patient with a postoperative fever: considering sources of infection, investigations, antimicrobial choices
  • Patient with postoperative anaemia: recognising if there is active bleeding or operative losses; when to initiate blood transfusion
  • Assess a surgical wound: recognising when healing is impaired (infection, haematoma, tension/dehiscence, ischaemia) and choose appropriate dressings
  • Handover of a patient before and after surgery: initiating appropriate prophylactic treatments, charting clinical history and medications, hand over to anaesthetics/ICU/recovery/afterhours team
  • Preparing a patient for discharge: rehabilitation requirements; discharge medications and analgesia instructions; wound care plans; surgical follow-up; GP liaison
